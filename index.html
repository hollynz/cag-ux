Our team is from Yoobee School of Design and has been working with the Christchurch Art Gallery to conduct user testing for their new website redesign prototype.

While the gallery’s website has been changed many times over the years, they have not had much feedback or testing to check that the changes they have made have been successful.

The main focus points for the gallery regarding this redesign are to ensure that specific information can be located easily to achieve better decentralisation of content, and to increase the number of physical visits to the gallery.

INTRODUCTION

- SLIDE 2

What We’ve Done

Over the course of 2 weeks, we conducted two user testing sessions: one at Christchurch Art Gallery, and one at Academy (a Christchurch education facility).

We split off into groups of two and used random sampling to test willing participants; one person drove the testing and the other observed and took notes as the participant used a laptop to test the prototype.

Each participant was asked some demographic questions and was then asked to complete a series of tasks to stress-test the prototype.

This website is a presentation of the results and insights gained from our user-testing sessions.

RESULTS

- SLIDE 1

Results

The general feeling from participants almost always began positively. Falloff was generally only seen once a participant began trying to interact with the site.

Our testing therefore showed that the fundamental building blocks of the prototype design were solid. If a participant located the intended pathway that led to the completion of a task, the rest became an exceptionally easy and smooth experience.

However, many participants faced difficulties during the tasks, and our data revealed some particular areas of concern for user experience:

Lack of informational and typographical hierarchy throughout the website
Design inconsistencies
Unsuccessful decentralisation, particularly of the home page

RESULTS

- SLIDE 2

Scoring

For all tested tasks, participants were scored on a scale of 1–3 in terms of their success level:

1: Task completed successfully with no struggles/confusion.

2: Task completed successfully but with some struggles/confusion.

3: Task uncompleted.

Therefore, the higher the score, the less successful a task. Taking the average scores across all participants shows that the tasks were fairly consistent in terms of success rates and that the tasks were generally not unsuccessful. However, the scores are still higher than is ideal, and further breaking the data as follows reveals some areas of concern for the prototype.

RESULTS

- SLIDE 4

Summary

Not all participants communicate their struggles or confusion. More specifically, computer-savvy users who take longer than they expect to find the information they are looking for tend not to describe themselves as confused/lost, instead remaining composed in order to complete a task.

Our observational data show that many such participants did struggle but could not verbalise why a task was not straightforward. Additionally, if people are having success in the tested tasks while showing confusion, this suggests issues with the following, which may be difficult to explain as a user:

"Rather than the placing of elements or how the indended pathways function, the issue lies in how certain links are highlighted and decentralised.

This leads to the general issue of a lack of text and information hierarchy, and those inconsistancies creating chaos for the user."

INSIGHTS

- SLIDE 1

Insights

As highlighted earlier, we have identified a few major reasons for the falloff in success rates. Given that the overall impressions for the prototype were positive and the placing of elements on the page seemed to be consistent with users’ expectations, our belief is that the problems can be grouped into the following:

Lack of informational and typographical hierarchy throughout the website
Design inconsistencies
Unsuccessful decentralisation, particularly of the home page

INSIGHTS

- SLIDE 2

Home Page Decentralisation


of participants commented that they felt the home page was cluttered

Our data and testing have shown that integrating the Bulletin magazine's content into the main pages of the website has been unsuccessful.

Decentralisation was a main focus point for the redesign, but improvements can be made. The main downfall is the Bulletin magazine content. Almost no participants understood what it was, but because of its very distinct look and lack of relevance to surrounding content, it caused significant confusion in several tested tasks.

We believe that there is certainly a place for this content to have high levels of hierarchy on the page, but its content needs to be redirected to a new page that can act as a stand alone for the Bulletin Magazine.

We suspect that if this one issue was dealt with correctly, the number of participants who expressed concern with clutter and confusion on the home page would be reduced dramatically.

INSIGHTS

- SLIDE 3

Typeface Hierarchy

The same typeface is used across all areas of the prototype

You can easily get away with a single typeface, but in that instance it is essential to use different font sizes and weights to create an easily understood visual hierarchy for new users.

There are inconsistencies regarding font sizes: corresponding elements are sized differently on different pages.

Inconsistencies in hierarchy make it much harder for users to know what to expect on any given page, leading to confusion and feelings of getting lost.

INSIGHTS

- SLIDE 4

Date Picker

Here we can see another issue with hierarchy.

When we have such an important information pathway starting at the date picker, it's extremely important that it's given the correct hierarchy.

This question was one of the most polarising. Participants either saw the date picker and completed the task exceptionally easily, or their attention was drawn else where and they were unable to complete the task at all. The black and white design base means elements with strong colours demand a huge amount of attention from the user. With the date picker not being given the correct amount of visual importance, many participants never found it.

INSIGHTS

- SLIDE 5

Newsletter

Very similarly to the datepicker, many participants struggled to find the newsletter due to it "blending into the rest of the navigation content".

Here we also see another example of a need for more decentralisation. 86% of participants commented that the home page was cluttered, with the majority of those being confused by the Bulletin section. Participants were regularly seen reading through the yellow section looking for the newsletter, not understanding that this section was actually completely irrelevant to what they were looking for.
